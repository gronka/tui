Start from the beginning, how is my property tax calculated? 

Basically, the amount of property tax you pay is based on the value of the property as determined by your town / city’s assessor’s office. The higher the value your assessor’s office places on your property, the more you will pay in property taxes. Contrary to popular understanding, your property is actually reassessed every year. Sometimes it’s obvious when this happens; if you recently purchased your home, the property will often be reassessed to match your purchase price. But sometimes it’s more subtle; your property’s assessed value may remain the exact same, but if the state feels your town / city is generally under assessed, the equalization rate may be lowered and your effective fair market value may actually increase (along with your taxes). To summarize, it’s a good idea to check your assessment every year to see if you can lower your property taxes. And Fairly Taxed can do that for you.

 

How do I know if my property is over assessed and I’m paying too much in property taxes? 

For residential properties, the key methods of valuation are a recent purchase of your property (if applicable) or recent sales of nearby, similar properties to your own. For commercial and industrial properties (including multifamily apartments complexes), the key valuations methods are a recent purchase of your property (if applicable) and the amount of income produced by the property. But there are nuances to each of these valuation methods, so if you are interested in successfully lowering your taxes to the greatest extent possible, we’d highly recommend that you allow Fairly Taxed’s expertise, data analysis and technology do the work.

 

What’s an assessment challenge / appeal, and should I bring one for my property?

As we mentioned, your property is reassessed every year. And every year property owners are given a very small window (usually less than a month) to challenge the amount your assessor’s office says your property is worth. That’s less than a month to (i) find your new assessment, (ii) assemble all of your evidence supporting a lower valuation and taxes, and (iii) fill out and submit a complicated state form with supporting documentation. In our opinion, this is done somewhat by design as most governments would prefer you simply pay the property taxes they tell you are due without questioning whether that amount is accurate or fair. One of the reasons we founded Fairly Taxed was to provide a service where property owners could easily achieve fair property taxes without having to spend hours upon hours of their own time or hiring an expensive attorney or consultant.

As to whether you should challenge your assessment, we’d encourage you to submit your property information through our website to find out. Once you enter your information onto our website, our property tax experts and algorithms will review your property and provide you feedback as to whether you should challenge your property taxes this year. We have an incredibly high success rate and aim to keep it there, so if we aren’t confident that we can lower your taxes, we will let you know.

But if you received a letter from us or we tell you that your property is over assessed, you could potentially save thousands of dollars a year by bringing a challenge. Could my taxes go up because I filed a challenge?

No. By law, your assessment and property taxes cannot be raised because you filed an assessment appeal.

 

Can I estimate the Market Value of my property without getting an expensive appraisal?

Yes. In fact, often times an appraisal will actually hurt your chances of receiving a reduced assessment. For this reason, we provide a comparable report (either as a standalone service or as part of our challenge service) which provides accurate and persuasive valuation tools for your property. If you have a recent appraisal of your property and are considering using it, we’d suggest checking with us first.
